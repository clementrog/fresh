# Fresh Editorial Doctrine

This doctrine defines what Fresh should consider worth noticing, worth keeping, worth enriching, and worth publishing.

It is not a voice guide, a sensitivity policy, or a formatting guide. It is an editorial compass.

Its job is to help every stage answer the same question:

**What does Linc care about enough to turn into editorial substance?**

---

## 1. Editorial scope

Linc writes first for **cabinets de paie** and **cabinets d’expertise comptable**.

The center of gravity is not generic HR content, generic SaaS commentary, or employer communication in the abstract. The relevant reader is the person who carries payroll production risk, client accountability, compliance pressure, migration risk, and service expectations inside a cabinet.

Fresh should therefore privilege signals related to:

- payroll production reality
- reliability and correctness
- calculation clarity and explicability
- traceability and auditability
- DSN and post-paie control
- migration without production paralysis
- client-cabinet collaboration
- support quality and operational trust
- portfolio management and production visibility
- concrete productivity gains for payroll teams
- tradeoffs and decisions that shape how cabinets work

A topic is in scope when it helps explain how payroll work is actually produced, controlled, secured, reviewed, or improved inside a cabinet.

---

## 2. What makes a signal worth retaining

A source item is worth retaining when it contains at least one of the following:

- a **real market tension**
- a **recurring customer objection**
- a **production friction** with operational consequences
- a **non-obvious learning** from a client call, immersion, support case, product decision, or market observation
- a **customer-visible implication** of an internal product or process decision
- a **proof point** that sharpens Linc’s market position
- a **change in workflow, risk, timing, cost, control, or trust**
- a **signal that repeats across cabinets**, conventions, migrations, or support situations
- a **precise formulation used by the field** that reveals how cabinets think, decide, or hesitate

Fresh should prefer source material that is anchored in lived reality over source material that is merely well-worded.

---

## 3. What makes a signal enrich-worthy

A retained signal becomes enrich-worthy when it is not just interesting, but reusable.

Fresh should enrich when the signal:

- can inform more than one future post or opportunity
- captures a pattern, not just an anecdote
- helps clarify **why something matters now**
- sharpens Linc’s understanding of the category, not just of one account
- reveals a stable tension in the market, product, workflow, or buying process
- can be expressed as a clear angle, contradiction, lesson, objection, or insight
- is specific enough to support editorial development later

Fresh should skip enrichment when the item is real but too narrow, too isolated, too tactical, or too context-bound to travel.

---

## 4. Quality bar for editorial substance

Not everything true is editorially useful.

For Linc, a piece of material clears the quality bar when it is:

### Specific
It contains a concrete fact, tension, workflow, objection, tradeoff, behavior, or consequence.

### Traceable
It can be tied to a real source: client call, immersion, product decision, support friction, internal data, market observation, or documented discussion.

### Consequential
It changes how a cabinet reader might think, decide, prioritize, migrate, control, explain, or organize payroll work.

### Non-generic
It teaches something that would not comfortably appear in any generic B2B SaaS content calendar.

### Position-sharpening
It helps explain what Linc sees in the market, what Linc rejects, what Linc is building around, or what cabinets actually need.

Fresh should systematically prefer:

- proof over opinion
- observations over slogans
- frictions over abstractions
- arbitrages over declarations
- customer-visible consequences over internal motion
- learned reality over category clichés

---

## 5. Editorial identity

Linc should sound like a company that understands payroll as a production system, not like a company trying to perform modernity.

The editorial identity is built around the following convictions:

- payroll deserves precision, not hand-waving
- reliability must be demonstrated, not claimed
- software should make calculations clearer, not more opaque
- workflows should increase control, not fragility
- support should reduce hesitation, not create dependence
- migration should be approached as an operational transition, not a marketing promise
- the strongest content comes from the field, not from generic brand narration

Fresh should therefore favor material that helps articulate Linc as a company concerned with:

- clarity
- traceability
- reliability
- operational calm
- trust
- accountability
- cabinet reality
- field-informed product choices

---

## 6. In-bounds themes

Thematically, Fresh should favor content about:

### Payroll production reality
What makes payroll work hard, fragile, stressful, repetitive, delayed, or difficult to control.

### Reliability and proof
How correctness is checked, explained, audited, traced, or restored.

### Calculation explicability
Anything that helps make payroll logic readable, understandable, and reviewable.

### DSN and control
DSN friction, DSN confidence, regularization logic, verification, editing, and operational consequences.

### Migration and adoption
What blocks migration, what creates trust, what makes a transition usable in real cabinet conditions.

### Cabinet economics and workload
Capacity constraints, profitability conditions, productivity gains, quality-risk tradeoffs, and what helps cabinets deliver more safely.

### Client service quality
Anything that affects response quality, clarity toward clients, visibility, turnaround time, and confidence.

### Product decisions with customer-visible consequences
Only when the decision changes the real work of cabinets or improves how payroll is produced, checked, explained, or delivered.

### Market structure and category tensions
Where the market is failing cabinets, where incumbents create distance or opacity, and what is changing in expectations around payroll software and service.

### Regulatory and operational change
Only when it changes practice, workload, risk, interpretation, or control for cabinets.

---

## 7. Out-of-bounds themes

Fresh should skip, downweight, or reject material that is primarily:

### Generic startup content
Founder energy, startup lessons, hiring vibes, company-building inspiration, or generic “building in public” without a payroll-specific consequence.

### Generic HR or future-of-work commentary
Broad workforce trends, HR transformation narratives, or management advice that do not materially help a cabinet payroll audience.

### Internal noise without external consequence
Project chatter, delivery movement, internal coordination, or technical status changes that do not affect customers, workflows, trust, or editorial understanding.

### Product marketing without operational substance
Feature mentions, launches, or improvements that do not explain what changes in practice, control, speed, reliability, or service.

### Generic advice
Tips, reminders, or observations that are true but obvious, unowned, and undifferentiated.

### Untraceable claims
Statements with no source, no proof, no grounding, or no meaningful consequence.

### Employer-first framing that bypasses the cabinet reality
Content that treats direct employers as the primary buyer or user unless the point is explicitly about how that reality affects the cabinet.

---

## 8. What Fresh should avoid thematically

Fresh should avoid content that:

- could be published by any B2B SaaS company with minor wording changes
- says “productivity” without showing what becomes easier, faster, safer, clearer, or more controllable
- says “innovation” where “control”, “clarity”, or “reliability” would be more truthful
- uses a product change as a story when the real story is missing
- over-indexes on brand messaging instead of real editorial substance
- treats market signals as content before understanding their operational meaning
- confuses novelty with relevance

If a source item is interesting but does not help explain cabinet reality, payroll reality, market reality, or a meaningful Linc learning, it should usually be skipped.

---

## 9. Source hierarchy

When multiple possible signals exist, Fresh should prefer source material in roughly this order:

1. direct field reality  
   client calls, immersion, objections, observed workflows, production friction

2. customer-visible product or support reality  
   incidents, tradeoffs, adoption blockers, migration patterns, repeated support issues

3. internal evidence with external meaning  
   documented learnings, internal data, repeated patterns, product decisions with customer consequences

4. market research  
   only when it sharpens editorial understanding rather than repeating public noise

Fresh should be skeptical of material that is well-phrased but weakly evidenced.

---

## 10. Decision heuristic

When in doubt, Fresh should ask:

### Retain
Does this item reveal something real about how cabinets buy, produce, control, migrate, explain, or experience payroll?

### Enrich
Does it contain a reusable pattern, angle, or lesson that could support future editorial work?

### Create
Is there enough specificity, consequence, and relevance to justify a standalone opportunity?

### Skip
Is it generic, internal-only, obvious, too narrow, weakly sourced, or disconnected from Linc’s editorial territory?

If a source item does not help Linc say something more precise, more grounded, or more useful than generic market commentary, it should usually be skipped.

---

## 11. Final standard

Linc should not publish because something is available to say.

Linc should publish when there is enough substance to say something that is:

- grounded
- specific
- useful
- differentiated
- traceable
- relevant to cabinet reality

**If it could come from any company, it is below the bar.  
If it has no real source, it is below the bar.  
If it says nothing new about the work, the market, or the tradeoffs, it is below the bar.**
